The idea of a trip

At home · on the web

You search Berlin to Paris, overnight. In seconds you see real departures, real prices, and exactly what's still free — a seat for the day, a berth or a private suite for the night.

YouSearch route & date→See live availability→See the real price
CompanyOne master record→Our apps + outside sellers→Same product everywhere
Company
The very same availability also feeds outside sellers like Trainline and Omio — so people can discover the train wherever they already shop, all drawing on one single source of truth for what's for sale.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
There's one master record of every seat, berth and price — so the number you see is never a stale guess, and you're never shown something that's already gone. Whether you book on our own site or through a third party, everyone reads from that same record, so the product never looks different in two places. (In the architecture: shared inventory & pricing behind an open industry standard, OSDM.)
2

Choosing your bed

Booking & payment

You pick your exact berth from the layout, sign in, and pay. A ticket with a scannable code lands instantly. The whole booking screen works by keyboard and screen-reader, so it's usable by everyone — not an afterthought.

YouPick your berth→Sign in→Pay→Get your ticket
CrewHold the track rights→Certify the train→Trip goes on sale
Company
You can sign in the European way — with Google, Apple, or your national government ID (like FranceConnect). It's a deliberately European product, and your privacy rights are built in from the start.
Crew
Before this train could even be sold, an operations planner confirmed the company holds the legal right to run it across all three countries' tracks — and that the train itself is certified to cross those borders. No right to run, no trip on sale.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
The moment you pay, your bed becomes truly yours. Taking the money and reserving the berth happen as a single locked step, so there's no way to be double-charged or double-booked — even if a thousand people are booking at once. Almost everything else in the system is built for speed and resilience; this one path is deliberately built for certainty. (In the architecture: strong consistency in payments & ticketing.)
3

Stepping aboard

On the platform · at the door

At the station, a conductor scans your code and welcomes you on. Your cabin is fresh, made up, ready — exactly as booked.

YouArrive at the platform→Code scanned→Welcomed aboard
CrewCabin cleaned→Marked ready in app→Train cleared to go
Crew
The conductor scans you in using the staff app on a handheld device — and it works even with no signal in the station. Earlier, a cleaner used the same app to mark your cabin turned-around and ready, which is how operations knew the train was good to go.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
Every train carries its own small brain on board. Before departure it pre-loads who's booked and which tickets are valid, so check-in, ticket scans and crew tasks all keep working with no connection at all — then catch up with the outside world whenever signal returns. (In the architecture: the Train Edge Gateway + the offline-first Operator App.)
4

The night on board

In your cabin · transportation meets hospitality

From your phone you dim the lights, set the temperature, and lock your cabin to private. You glance at the app to see the bathroom's free before getting up, and order breakfast for the morning. It feels less like a train and more like a small hotel that happens to be moving.

YouOpen the app→Set lights & warmth→Lock cabin private→Order breakfast
CrewOrder hits the galley→Prepared→Served in the morning
Crew
Your breakfast order appears on the on-board host's app in the galley, queued and ready to prepare — the same order you placed from your bunk.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
Your phone talks directly to the train for anything physical — lights, temperature, the privacy lock. And “is the bathroom free?” is answered live and then forgotten instantly — that kind of status is never stored anywhere, on purpose, as a privacy line we won't cross. (In the architecture: a device-shadow IoT layer; facility state is real-time only, never persisted.)
5

Somewhere past midnight, the signal drops

A long dark stretch between cities

The train loses its connection to the outside world. You don't notice. Your lights still respond. Your door stays locked. Your ticket still shows. The bathroom indicator still updates. When signal returns an hour later, everything you and the crew did quietly syncs back up — nothing lost, nothing doubled.

YouSignal drops→Everything keeps working→Syncs on reconnect
CrewKeep scanning & logging→Held safely on board→Reconciled instantly
Crew
The conductor kept checking tickets and the cleaner kept logging tasks the whole time. Their work was held safely on board and reconciled the instant the train reconnected.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
This is the hardest problem in the whole platform, and the reason it exists. Connectivity on a moving train is unreliable, so nothing important is allowed to depend on a live connection. The train's brain holds the truth locally and merges cleanly with the cloud when reconnected. It's being proven on a real physical test rig — model trains in a lab — years before the real trains arrive. (In the architecture: offline-first sync-on-reconnect, validated in the Lab.)
6

Arriving in Paris

Morning · trip complete

You wake rested, breakfast arrives, the trip quietly marks itself complete. You step off into the city having slept your way across a continent.

YouArrive in Paris→Breakfast→Trip complete
CompanyNight's events collected→Walled-off analytics→Better routes & rewards
Company
Every booking, scan, meal and cabin tweak from the night becomes insight the company learns from — to plan routes, price fairly, and reward loyal travellers. Analysts see the patterns; they never see your name or your cabin controls. That wall is built in.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
The reporting world is walled off from the live service entirely — a separate, read-only place where the business looks at trends, never at the controls that run real trips or at personal data it shouldn't touch. (In the architecture: a separate data warehouse with read-only, role-scoped access.)

One platform, three lives served at once

Across that single night, the same system quietly handled the traveller's comfort, the crew's work, and the company's understanding of its business — without any of them getting in each other's way.

The bigger picture

A new morning, and the whole machine in one view. Everything you just lived through runs on a single platform — and every app is simply a different door into the same secure core. The train carries its own piece of it, which is why life on board never depends on a signal.

Solid lines are the everyday connections every app makes to the platform — including the passenger web app. The dashed line is unique to the passenger mobile app: it can talk straight to the train, the link that keeps your cabin, ticket and facilities working when there's no signal, then catches up with the platform once there is.
