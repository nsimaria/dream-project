1 The idea of a trip · At home, on the web

The idea of a trip

You search Berlin to Paris, overnight. In seconds you see real departures, real prices, and exactly what's still free — a seat for the day, a berth or a private suite for the night.

PassengerSearch route & date→See live availability→See the real price
CompanyOne master record→Our apps + outside sellers→Same product everywhere
Company
The very same availability also feeds outside sellers like Trainline and Omio — so people can discover the train wherever they already shop, all drawing on one single source of truth for what's for sale.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
There's one master record of every seat and berth — so you're never shown something that's already gone, and the number you see is never a stale guess. The price isn't a fixed entry in a table: a dynamic pricing engine sets each fare live from real demand and what's still free, so it reflects the moment. Whether you book on our own site or through a third party, everyone reads from that same live record, so the product never looks different in two places. (In the architecture: shared inventory and a dynamic pricing engine, opened to partners through an open industry standard, OSDM.)
2 Choosing your bed · Booking & payment

Choosing your bed

You pick your exact berth from the layout, sign in, and pay. A ticket with a scannable code lands instantly.

PassengerPick your berth→Sign in→Pay→Get your ticket
CrewHold the track rights→Certify the train→Trip goes on sale
Company
You can sign in the European way — with Google, Apple, or your national government ID (like FranceConnect). It's a deliberately European product, and your privacy rights are built in from the start.
Crew
Before this train could even be sold, an operations planner confirmed the company holds the legal right to run it across all three countries' tracks — and that the train itself is certified to cross those borders. No right to run, no trip on sale.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
The moment you pay, your bed becomes truly yours. Taking the money and reserving the berth happen as a single locked step, so there's no way to be double-charged or double-booked — even if a thousand people are booking at once. Almost everything else in the system is built for speed and resilience; this one path is built for certainty. (In the architecture: strong consistency in payments & ticketing.)
3 Stepping aboard · On the platform, at the door

Stepping aboard

At the station, a conductor scans your code and welcomes you on. Your cabin is fresh, made up, ready — exactly as booked.

PassengerArrive at the platform→Code scanned→Welcomed aboard
CrewScan guests at the door→Settle them aboard→Live passenger manifest
CrewCabin cleaned→Marked ready in app→Train cleared to go
Crew
The conductor scans you in using the staff app on a handheld device — and it works even with no signal in the station. Earlier, a cleaner used the same app to mark your cabin turned-around and ready, which is how operations knew the train was good to go.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
Every train carries its own small brain on board. Before departure it pre-loads who's booked and which tickets are valid, so check-in, ticket scans and crew tasks all keep working with no connection at all — then catch up with the outside world whenever signal returns. (In the architecture: the Train Edge Gateway + the offline-first Operator App.)
4 The night on board · Transportation meets hospitality

The night on board

From your phone you dim the lights, set the temperature, and lock your cabin to private. You glance at the app to see the bathroom's free before getting up, and order breakfast for the morning. It feels less like a train and more like a small hotel that happens to be moving.

PassengerOpen the app→Set lights & warmth→Lock cabin private→Order breakfast
CrewOrder hits the galley→Prepared→Served in the morning
Crew
Your breakfast order appears on the on-board host's app in the galley, queued and ready to prepare — the same order you placed from your bunk.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
Your phone talks directly to the train for anything physical — lights, temperature, the privacy lock. And "is the bathroom free?" is answered live. (In the architecture: device-shadow IoT layer.)
5 Somewhere past midnight · A long dark stretch between cities

Somewhere past midnight, the signal drops

The train loses its connection to the outside world. You don't notice. Your lights still respond. Your door stays locked. Your ticket still shows. The bathroom indicator still updates. When signal returns an hour later, everything you and the crew did quietly syncs back up — nothing lost, nothing doubled.

PassengerSignal drops→Everything keeps working→Syncs on reconnect
CrewKeep scanning & logging→Held safely on board→Reconciled instantly
Crew
The conductor kept checking tickets and the cleaner kept logging tasks the whole time. Their work was held safely on board and reconciled the instant the train reconnected.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
This is one of the hardest problems to solve in the whole platform. Connectivity on a moving train is unreliable, so nothing important is allowed to depend on a live connection. The train's brain holds the truth locally and merges cleanly with the cloud when reconnected. It's being proven on a real physical test rig — model trains in a lab — years before the real trains arrive. (In the architecture: offline-first sync-on-reconnect, validated in the Lab.)
6 Arriving in Paris · Morning, trip complete

Arriving in Paris

You wake rested, breakfast arrives, the trip quietly marks itself complete. You step off into the city having slept your way across a continent.

PassengerArrive in Paris→Breakfast→Trip complete
CompanyNight's events collected→Walled-off analytics→Better routes & rewards
Company
Every booking, scan, meal and cabin tweak from the night becomes insight the company learns from — to plan routes, price fairly, and reward loyal travellers. Analysts see the patterns; they never see your name or your cabin controls. That wall is built in.
⚙ Behind the scenes ›
The reporting world is walled off from the live service entirely — a separate, read-only place where the business looks at trends, never at the controls that run real trips or at personal data it shouldn't touch. (In the architecture: a separate data warehouse with read-only, role-scoped access.)

8 The bigger picture

The bigger picture

Everything you just lived through runs on a single platform — and every app is simply a different door into the same secure core. The train carries its own piece of it, which is why life on board never depends on a signal.

Solid lines are the everyday connections every app makes to the platform — including the passenger web app. The dashed lines are the on-board links that talk straight to the train: the passenger mobile app keeps your cabin, ticket and facilities working when there's no signal, while the crew's operator app drives check-in and onboard tasks the same way — both catching up with the platform once signal returns.

9 Inside the platform · the secure core, opened up

Inside the platform

The same single block from the last slide, opened up. Each audience — passengers, operators, partners, corporate — reaches its own surface, which enters through its own checked door, then talks to a set of focused services — each owning its own data — all connected by one durable spine. Reporting lives walled off to the side.

The gold-edged services — ticketing and payments — are the two held to strict, no-double-booking consistency; everything else trades a little strictness for resilience and speed. The hub on the right reaches out to the rail networks and payment providers, and the train carries its own slice of the platform, which is why life on board survives a lost signal.

10 De-risking the work · figuring out the hard things first

De-risking the work

We have years before a real train — so we don't wait for one. We practise the whole system on progressively more realistic stand-ins, and we attack the hardest unknowns up front, in software, where a wrong answer is cheap to change.

MVP — the hard things firstOffline sync & conflict resolutionNo double-book, no double-chargeThe whole ride, end to end in the Lab

The expensive, irreversible decisions get made only after the cheap, reversible ones have taught us the answer — so by the time a real carriage exists, the platform has already lived the journey thousands of times. The real train is the final check, not where we discover problems.

11 Proving the economics · a market simulator for revenue management

Proving the economics

The same simulators that de-risk the engineering do a second job: we point them at the business. Thousands of synthetic travellers book through the real pricing and inventory path — across seasons, day and night, and every leg of the route — so we test and tune revenue management long before a single ticket is sold, and prove each train pays for itself from day one.

Validated in simulationForecast accuracyPricing strategy, A/B-testedDay vs night capacityMargin per departure

Because the booking, pricing and inventory under test are the real ones — not a spreadsheet model — what works in simulation is what ships. We reach launch with pricing already tuned and the unit economics already proven, rather than discovering them on real passengers.
